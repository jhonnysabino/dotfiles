# Business Idea Validation — Reference

## Stage 0 — Define What You Want

### Pain vs Enjoyment Businesses

**Pain businesses** solve a customer frustration:
- HubSpot — pain of tracking sales data
- DoorDash — pain of picking up food
- Marketing consultancy — pain of managing ads
- Pool service — pain of manual labor

**Enjoyment businesses** provide entertainment:
- Warner Bros — movies
- Riot Games — video games
- Instagram — social media
- Restaurants — dining experience

### Why Pain Businesses Win
1. **Pain drives payment.** Each experience of pain pushes the customer toward paying.
2. **Pain returns → loyalty.** Recurring pain = recurring customer.
3. **Enjoyment competes with all enjoyment.** A game competes with Netflix, social media, sports, etc.
4. **Enjoyment needs more work.** Must be exceptionally good to win attention.

**You can build a great enjoyment business.** Just make it a conscious decision — the path is harder.

### Know Your Constraints
- Time available per week
- Runway / financial buffer
- Lifestyle requirements (remote, specific hours, etc.)
- Risk tolerance

## Stage 1 — Self Skillset Evaluation

### Tier List Template

| Tier | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Good** | Someone would pay you to do this, even at junior level |
| **Acceptable** | You can do it; with practice could go pro |
| **Bad** | You suck at this or haven't done much |

### Skills to Evaluate (add your own)
Sales, Graphic Design, Product Design, Content Creation, Marketing (ad platforms, SEO), Public Speaking, Technical Literacy (Zapier, site builders), Programming, Finance, Operational Efficiency, Leadership

### The Business Idea Venn Diagram
Your likelihood of success ∝ overlap between **skills the business needs** and **your Good skills**.

- High overlap → strong position
- Low overlap in critical areas → find co-founder or pick different idea

### Example: Troy 15 years ago
- Good: Programming, Technical Literacy, Public Speaking, Leadership
- Bad: Sales, Marketing, Finance, Operations
- **Bad fit**: SEO SaaS (needs Sales + Marketing as core skills)
- **Good fit**: Game server hosting (needs Programming + Technical Literacy)

## Stage 2 — Identify Opportunities

### Path A: Chase The Pain

1. **Document pain points** — from your life, job, family, friends. Keep a living document.
2. **Don't jump to solutions yet** — just capture the pain clearly.
3. **Rate each pain point:**

| Dimension | Scale | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| Pain level | 1–5 | How much does this suck? |
| Frequency | 1–5 | How often does it happen? |
| Niche | 1–5 | How specific is the audience? (5 = very niche) |

4. **Why niche wins:**
   - Less competition with true product-market fit
   - Easier to charge premium (tailor-built for underserved group)
   - Direct feedback loops are tighter
   - Growth loops work better inside niches
   - Expanding from niche → broad is easier than broad → niche

**There is no "too niche."** If pain is significant, small market can still be worth it.

### Path B: Revenue-First (SaaS-specific)

1. **Go to TrustMRR or AppSumo.** Filter by category.
2. **Look for products at $20K+ MRR.** Market exists.
3. **You're not looking for inspiration** — you're looking for proof of demand.
4. **Boring categories with consistent revenue > exciting categories with no signal.**

## Stage 3 — Research

### Competition Analysis

**You want to find competition.** No competition = red flag (market likely doesn't exist).

Exceptions (confirm at least one):
- Brand-new technology (find tangential products)
- Extremely niche space (research adjacent niches)

**Questions to answer for each competitor:**

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Pricing strategy? | What market pays. Subscription vs one-time |
| Size & how they grew? | Employees, funding, bootstrapped? |
| How do they frame the solution? | Landing page copy, differentiators |
| How open about price? | Signals price sensitivity of market |

### Review Gap Analysis (Material 2)

**Pattern to hunt**: Product at $30K MRR, 3.4★, 40+ reviews.

Meaning: Customers exist and are paying, but **nobody has nailed it**. Read the 2-star reviews — that's your product roadmap.

Red flags:
- 4.5★+ across all competitors → market is well-served, hard to differentiate
- No reviews anywhere → market may not exist
- All reviews complain about price → race to bottom

### Growth Signal Check (Material 2)

Revenue is lagging data. Growth rate tells you where demand is heading.

**+30% MoM in a boring niche beats $500K MRR flat.**

How to estimate:
- TrustMRR shows growth trends if available
- Job postings (hiring fast = growing)
- Social following growth
- Product update frequency

### Reddit Pain Validation (Material 2)

**Search the problem, not the solution.**

Bad search: "best SEO tool for agencies"
Good search: "I spend 3 hours a week manually..."

800 upvotes on a pain post > 10 survey responses.

Check:
- r/startups, r/smallbusiness, r/entrepreneur
- Niche subreddits for your target market
- Look for recurring complaints, not one-off rants

### Customer Discovery

| Question | How to answer |
|---|---|
| Who are customers? | From niche evaluation |
| Where are they? | Online communities, events, platforms |
| Time to find 1 potential customer? | If hours → hard. If minutes → easy |

### Marketing or Sales Company?

**Most startups are one or the other early on. Rarely both.**

| Marketing Company | Sales Company |
|---|---|
| Low price / small deal size | High price / large deal size |
| Simple self-setup | Complex setup |
| No contracts | Contracts or volume commitments |
| Selling to individuals | Selling to businesses |
| Easily targetable audience | Relationship-driven |

**Critical filter**: If space needs a sales company but price is too low to pay sales commissions → bad business.

### TAM / SAM / SOM (Quick Version)

Don't do a full evaluation at this stage. Quick napkin math only.

| Metric | Formula |
|---|---|
| TAM | # people in space × price × frequency/year |
| SAM | # people in need × price × frequency/year |
| SOM | SAM × 10% |

**Always anchor assumptions on the low end.**

**Timebox: 10 hours per opportunity maximum.** Set a timer. Ship the research, don't polish it.

## Stage 4 — Categorize & Pick

### Scoring Table (Google Sheets)

| Opportunity | Timeline to Ship | Opportunity Size | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Idea A | <1 month | Mid (<$1M) | Fair |
| Idea B | 1-3 months | Large (<$10M) | High |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Timeline to Ship
- **<1 month**: Simple, few dependencies, uses your Good skills
- **1-3 months**: Moderate complexity, some learning curve
- **3+ months**: Complex, steep learning curve, or lots of dependencies

**If most ideas are 3+ months → you're overbuilding. Think leaner.**

### Opportunity Size (based on SOM)
- **Low**: <$500K
- **Mid**: <$1M
- **Large**: <$10M
- **Venture**: >$10M (VC-interest territory)

### Confidence
- **Low (<10%)**: Wild guess, unvalidated assumptions
- **Fair (10-50%)**: Some signals, but gaps remain
- **Likely (50-80%)**: Strong signals, you'd use the product yourself
- **High (>80%)**: You are the customer, clear demand signals

### Decision Heuristics

- **Short timeline → lower confidence acceptable.** Quick to test, quick to fold.
- **Bigger market ≠ better.** More competition, more resources needed.
- **Venture scale needs high confidence.** Don't chase VC-scale ideas on a hunch.
- **If you'd buy it yourself → confidence jumps significantly.**
- **No formula.** Use context. Make a decision. Don't get stuck.

## Stage 5 — Test Market

### The Leanest Possible Solution

**This is not an MVP. It's simpler.**

Examples of "leanest possible solution":
- Google Form + manual work behind the scenes
- Concierge MVP (you do it manually for each customer)
- Landing page with email waitlist + manual demos
- Spreadsheet + phone calls
- Existing tools hacked together

**Rule**: Anything not directly addressing the core pain → cut it.

### Goal: 10 Paying Customers

Exact number depends on product type, but 10 is right for most.

What you're validating:
1. **Were assumptions correct?** Did research match reality?
2. **Can you get customers?** How hard is it to get someone to pay?
3. **What do customers actually want?** Feedback will reshape your solution.

### Pricing Tactics for First Customers

- **Sell at cost.** Break even, learn what they value.
- **Godfather offer.** Aggressive price locked in long-term for early adopters.
- **Free trial with payment info.** Trial period with agreed charge date. Better than free.
- **Never free.** Free = bad feedback. People don't value what they don't pay for.

### Finding First Customers

**Lean into being early-stage.** Don't fake professionalism.

- Approach with humble eagerness to help
- People root for founders who are transparent
- Direct founder-to-customer conversations = advantage big companies can't replicate

### Advisor Shares
- 0.1–0.5% total equity for early customers (especially prestigious groups: lawyers, doctors, execs)
- Frame in number of shares (bigger number = more appealing)
- Triggers IKEA effect — people love what they helped build
- Turns customers into long-term feedback sources and advocates

### Know When to Fold

**Red flags — kill the idea:**
- 100 conversations → zero interest
- Paying customers only come from friends/family
- Feedback consistently says "nice to have" not "must have"
- You dread working on it
- Competitor with 10× your resources enters and you can't differentiate

**"Never give up" applies to being an entrepreneur, not to specific ideas.** Kill ideas fast. Preserve your time.

---

## Stage 6 — Commit

Once you have:
- 10 paying customers
- Validated assumptions
- Clear feedback loop
- Conviction this is worth pursuing

**Stop exploring other ideas.** Your limited resource is time. Go all in.
